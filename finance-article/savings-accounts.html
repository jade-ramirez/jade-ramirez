Understanding Savings Accounts

Last edited: 7/5/2025

Savings Accounts in the Financial System

Before diving into the specifics of savings accounts, it's essential to understand their role within the broader financial system. Banks serve as crucial financial intermediariesInstitutions that facilitate the flow of funds between savers and borrowers.. They connect those with surplus funds (savers) to those who need capital (borrowers), such as individuals taking out mortgages or businesses seeking loans for expansion. This channeling of funds is fundamental to economic growth, allowing capital to flow where it's most productive.

Why We Have FDIC Insurance

The concept of pooling deposits and lending them out, while beneficial for the economy, comes with inherent risks. Historically, a major vulnerability of banking systems was the "bank run." This occurs when a large number of depositors simultaneously attempt to withdraw their money, fearing the bank might fail. Since banks only hold a fraction of deposits in reserve (more on this below), they cannot meet all withdrawal demands at once. Prior to the 1930s, bank runs were a frequent and devastating occurrence, often triggered by economic downturns or a loss of public trust. The widespread bank failures during the Great Depression led to immense financial chaos and a near-collapse of the U.S. financial system.

In response, the U.S. government established the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) in 1933. The primary purpose of FDIC insurance is to protect depositors' money in the event of a bank failure. Today, the FDIC insures deposits up to $250,000 per depositor, per insured bank, per ownership category [1]. This guarantee instills crucial trust and confidenceThe belief that one's money is safe in a bank, essential for preventing bank runs. in the banking system. When depositors know their money is safe, they are far less likely to panic and withdraw funds en masse, preventing systemic instability. This mechanism has proven its worth, even in recent financial events like the Silicon Valley Bank collapse in 2023, where FDIC's swift action helped contain broader panic.

Fractional Reserve Banking: How Banks Make Money

The foundation of modern banking is known as fractional reserve bankingA banking system in which banks hold only a fraction of deposits in reserve and lend out the remainder.. When you deposit money into a savings account, the bank doesn't just hold all of it in a vault. Instead, they are only required to hold a fraction of that deposit as reserves (either as physical cash or as deposits at the central bank). The vast majority of the money is then lent out to other customers in the form of loans (mortgages, business loans, personal loans, etc.).

This process is how banks generate profit and, importantly, how money is created in the economy. When a bank makes a loan, it essentially adds the borrower's account, creating new digital money. This loaned money is then spent by the borrower and eventually deposited into another bank, where a portion is again held in reserve, and the rest is lent out. This cycle, often called the "money multiplier effect," allows the initial deposit to lead to a much larger expansion of the money supply. While commercial banks are the primary creators of money through lending, central banks like the Federal Reserve control the base money supply and influence the overall money supply through various monetary policy tools, predominantly by setting target interest rates.

Banks profit from this system through what's called the Net Interest Margin (NIM). This is the difference between the interest they earn on the loans they issue (which is typically higher) and the interest they pay out on deposits (like your savings account interest). By "borrowing short" (taking your deposits, which are often demandable on short notice) and "lending long" (issuing longer-term loans), banks aim to profit from the spread between short-term and long-term interest rates.

How This Results in Higher Savings Account Interest Rates

So, how does this all tie back to the interest rate you earn on your savings account?

  1. Cost of Funds: The interest rate offered on your savings account is essentially the cost for the bank to acquire funds that they can then lend out at a higher rate. They need your deposits to fuel their lending activities and earn their Net Interest Margin.
  2. Central Bank Influence: The most significant factor influencing savings account rates is the central bank's monetary policy, particularly its target for short-term interest rates (like the Federal Funds Rate in the U.S.). When the central bank raises this rate to combat inflation or cool an overheating economy, it becomes more expensive for banks to borrow money from each other. Consequently, banks often raise the rates they offer on savings accounts to attract deposits, as these become a relatively cheaper source of funds than interbank borrowing. Conversely, when the central bank lowers rates, savings account rates tend to fall.
  3. Competition: Banks constantly compete for your deposits. The rise of online-only banks has intensified this competition. With lower overhead costs (no physical branches to maintain), online banks can often afford to offer significantly higher interest rates on their savings accounts, often termed "high-yield savings accounts." This competitive pressure forces traditional banks to offer more attractive rates, though often still lower than their online counterparts.
  4. Market Conditions and Bank Strategy: Broader economic conditions, inflation expectations, and the rates on other safe investments (like U.S. Treasury bills) also influence what banks offer. Banks also tailor their rates based on their specific liquidity needs and overall business strategy.

In essence, the interest you earn on your savings account is a reflection of the bank's need for deposits to fuel its lending operations, influenced by central bank policy, inter-bank competition, and the overall economic landscape.

Types of Savings Accounts and Their Trade-offs

As you consider where to keep your savings, it's important to understand the different types of accounts and the trade-offs they offer in terms of liquidity, returns, and risk. While all FDIC-insured accounts prioritize the safety of your principal up to the insured limit, their accessibility and earning potential can vary significantly. When to Pick Saving Over Investing

Choosing between saving and investing really depends on your goals and risk toleranceYour ability and willingness to lose some or all of your original investment in exchange for potential higher returns.. The circumstances of this decision can help determine which type of savings account is best. If perhaps you are saving up for a car, but you know that your current car will last you another 3 years then savings accounts might be the better option. Generally under 5 years is too soon to invest, but you can take advantage of more restrictive savings accounts such as a CD or money market account. Things to consider when choosing between saving and investing include:

  • Time Horizon: If you need the money in the short term (less than 5 years), saving is usually the better option. For longer-term goals (5+ years), investing can yield higher returns.
  • Risk Tolerance: If you are risk-averse and cannot afford to lose your principal, saving is safer. If you can tolerate some risk for potentially higher returns, investing may be suitable.
  • Liquidity Needs: If you need quick access to your funds, savings accounts are more liquid. Investments may take time to sell without incurring losses.
